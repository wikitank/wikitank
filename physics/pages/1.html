Descriptions of motion

Scalars & Vectors

Motion can be expressed through mainly two different manners, scalars and vectors. A scalar represents just a normal one-dimensional ($\mathbb{R}$) number, which is to say numbers like $1, 1.5, \pi, 4, \frac{3}{2},$ etc.

A vector on the other hand, is a 2-dimensional ($\mathbb{R}^2$) number, meaning that it will have both some magnitude and a direction, which is useful in many cases for describing motion. In this course any vector $\vec{v}$ will be denoted as:

$$\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}$$

Where $x$ represents a scalar valued displacement in the $x$-direction from some origin point (usually $\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}$), and vice-verca for $y$. Plotting the vector $\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}4\\3\end{bmatrix}$ on a pair of axes would look something like:

$x$ $y$
> $\vec{v}$

Since the components of a vector form some sort of "right-angled triangle", the magnitude (or length, denoted $\lVert\vec{v}\rVert$), which is a scalar value, of some vector $\vec{v}$ can be evaluated through pytahgoras theorem:

$\lVert\vec{v}\rVert$ $x$ $y$
$$\lVert\vec{v}\rVert=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$$

The difference

Imagine someone walks once along the circular path depicted as follows from the starting point marked in red:

$5m$

How far have they walked? Well, in terms of distance, they've walked the circumference of the circle, which is:

$$l=2\pi r=10\pi\approx 31.4m$$

But in terms of displacement (a vector), they haven't even walked at all! But why? Well a vector uses magnitude and direction to describe the movement from one point to another, and they've walked from the starting point to, ...the starting point! The magnitude of displacement (distance) inbetween the starting point and the starting point is $0m$, and the angle is $0^\circ$, so:

$$\vec{d}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}$$

Be careful not to accidentaly mix distance and displacement up!
