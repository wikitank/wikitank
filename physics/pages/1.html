Descriptions of motion

Motion can be expressed through mainly two different manners, scalars and vectors. A scalar represents just a normal one-dimensional ($\mathbb{R}$), which is to say numbers like $1, 1.5, \pi, 4, \frac{3}{2},$ etc.

A vector on the other hand, is a 2-dimensional ($\mathbb{R}^2$) number, meaning that it will have both some magnitude and a direction, which is useful in many cases for describing motion. In this course any vector $\vec{v}$ will be denoted as:

$$\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}$$

Where $x$ represents a scalar valued displacement in the $x$-direction from some origin point (usually $\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}$), and vice-verca for $y$.

Since the components of a vector form some sort of "right-angled triangle", the magnitude (or length), which is a scalar, of some vector $\vec{v}$ can be evaluated through pytahgoras theorem:

$\lVert\vec{v}\rVert$ $x$ $y$
$$\lVert\vec{v}\rVert=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$$

Imagine someone walks along the circular path depicted as follows from the starting point marked in red:

$5m$

How far have they walked? Well, in terms of distance, they've walked the circumference of the circle, which is:

$$l=2\pi r=10\pi\approx 31.4m$$

But in terms of displacement (a vector), they haven't even walked at all! But why? Well a vector has a magnitude and a vector to describe the movement from one point to another, and they've walked from the starting point to, ...the starting point! The magnitude (distance) inbetween the starting point and the starting point is $0m$, and the angle is $0^\circ$, so:

$$\vec{d}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}$$

So be careful not to accidentaly mix them up!
